✓ Normal

Normal urine sediment findings — expected, non-reportable

Normal Urine Sediment
Clean catch, midstream specimen
A normal sediment may contain rare squamous epithelial cells (from genital tract), a few hyaline casts (physiologic), and up to 5 WBCs/hpf and 3 RBCs/hpf. No bacteria, no casts other than hyaline, no crystals of pathologic significance.
WBC: 0–5/hpf RBC: 0–3/hpf Hyaline cast: occasional OK Squamous: rare OK
→ Report: Within normal limits / No significant findings
Hyaline Cast
Pale, homogenous, protein cylinder — Tamm-Horsfall protein
The only cast that can be present in health. Formed from Tamm-Horsfall mucoprotein in the collecting duct. Increased by dehydration, exercise, fever, or diuretics. View under reduced light — nearly transparent.
Occasional = normalMany = dehydration, diuretics
↑ Many: dehydration, concentrated urine, vigorous exercise
✕ Abnormal Cells

Reportable cellular findings in urine sediment

WBCs (Pyuria)
Neutrophils predominate; granular, multi-lobed nucleus
Neutrophils in urine indicate inflammation or infection. >5 WBC/hpf = pyuria. In glomerulonephritis, lymphocytes/monocytes may predominate. Eosinophils → allergic interstitial nephritis. Confirm with culture.
>5/hpf = pyuria WBC cast → pyelonephritis
Report: >5 WBC/hpf — correlate with LE, nitrite, culture
RBCs (Hematuria)
Isomorphic = lower tract; Dysmorphic = glomerular origin
Isomorphic: Uniform biconcave discs → lower urinary tract (stones, tumor, BPH, trauma). Dysmorphic / acanthocytic: Irregular, crenated, "Mickey Mouse" shapes → glomerulonephritis. Acanthocytes (>5%) are most specific for glomerular bleeding.
>3/hpf = hematuria Dysmorphic + RBC cast → GN
Report: >3 RBC/hpf; note dysmorphic if present
Squamous Epithelial Cells
Squamous (large) · Transitional · Renal Tubular (RTE)
Large polygonal cells, minimal clinical impact
Many squamous = contamination RTE cells → tubular injury
RTE cells: correlate with protein, casts, renal function
Transitional / Urothelial Cells
Squamous (large) · Transitional · Renal Tubular (RTE)
Rounder bladder/urothelial cells; may increase with irritation/infection
Many squamous = contamination RTE cells → tubular injury
RTE cells: correlate with protein, casts, renal function
Renal Tubular Epithelial Cells
Squamous (large) · Transitional · Renal Tubular (RTE)
Small clinically significant epithelial cells seen with tubular injury
Many squamous = contamination RTE cells → tubular injury
RTE cells: correlate with protein, casts, renal function
Bacteria
Rods / cocci in sediment
True bacteriuria on urine microscopy. Correlate with pyuria, symptoms, nitrite/leukocyte esterase, and urine culture. Report bacteria present and morphology if visible; culture is still required for organism identification.
Bacterial morphology only; culture required Pyuria supports infection
Report bacteria present / morphology if visible; culture + sensitivities required for ID
Yeast
Budding yeast / pseudohyphae
Yeast in urine sediment, often budding with or without pseudohyphae. Consider candiduria versus contamination; correlate with catheter status, symptoms, and culture before calling infection.
Candida pattern if budding / pseudohyphae Correlate clinically before calling infection
Report yeast present; correlate with symptoms, catheter status, and culture if needed
✕ Abnormal Casts

Casts form in renal tubules — their composition indicates the site and type of pathology

Granular Cast
Fine or coarse granules — degenerated cells
Degenerated cellular debris from WBC or RTE cells. Fine granules → early degeneration; coarse granules → further breakdown. Progresses to waxy cast. AKI, glomerulonephritis, interstitial nephritis.
AKI · GN · Interstitial nephritis
WBC Cast
Neutrophils embedded in matrix — pyelonephritis hallmark
Neutrophils trapped in Tamm-Horsfall protein matrix. Pathognomonic for pyelonephritis. Also: interstitial nephritis, lupus nephritis, acute GN. Confirms upper urinary tract origin of pyuria.
PyelonephritisInterstitial nephritis
RBC Cast
Red-orange cast — pathognomonic for glomerulonephritis
RBCs trapped in protein cylinder. Pathognomonic for glomerulonephritis (GN). Red-orange color from hemoglobin. Also: vasculitis, endocarditis, lupus nephritis. The most important cast to recognize.
GlomerulonephritisVasculitis
Waxy / Broad Cast
Wide, sharp edges, homogeneous, cracks — advanced renal disease
Highly refractile, waxy appearance with sharp edges and sometimes cracks. Forms in dilated tubules during oliguria/anuria. "Broad" cast = formed in collecting duct. Sign of advanced/end-stage renal disease, CKD, oliguric AKI.
CKD / ESRDOliguric AKI
Oval Fat Bodies / Fatty Cast
Maltese cross pattern under polarized light
Renal tubular cells or macrophages filled with lipid droplets. Show classic Maltese cross birefringence under polarized light. Present in nephrotic syndrome (>3.5 g/day proteinuria). Also: diabetic nephropathy, lupus nephritis, mercury poisoning.
Nephrotic syndromeMaltese cross (polarized)
Cast Progression

Cellular → Granular → Waxy
↑ severity of tubular injury
Cast Hierarchy
Cellular → Granular → Waxy → Broad
Casts represent a spectrum of tubular injury. Cellular (freshest, most specific) → granular (degeneration) → waxy/broad (most severe, chronic stasis). Find the most abnormal cast present and report it.
Report most abnormal cast
✕ Crystals

Identify shape + pH context — most are incidental, some are pathologic

Calcium Oxalate
Envelope (dihydrate) · Dumbbell (monohydrate)
Most common crystal. Envelope/octahedral (dihydrate) or dumbbell-shaped (monohydrate). Seen in acidic or neutral urine. Most common crystal in kidney stones. Pathologic in large numbers; ethylene glycol poisoning produces massive monohydrate oxalate crystals.
Common; pathologic if massiveEG poisoning if monohydrate floods
Triple Phosphate (Struvite)
Coffin-lid prism — alkaline urine, UTI
Rectangular prism with oblique ends resembling a "coffin lid." Found only in alkaline urine. Associated with UTI caused by urea-splitting organisms (Proteus, Klebsiella). Can form "staghorn calculi."
Alkaline urine + UTIStaghorn calculi risk
Uric Acid
Yellow-brown rhomboid / rosette — acidic urine
Yellow-brown rhomboids, rods, or rosette formations. Found only in acidic urine (pH <5.5). Gout, hyperuricemia, tumor lysis syndrome, high-protein diet. Dissolve in alkaline urine (a useful confirmatory test).
Acidic urine, goutTumor lysis syndrome
